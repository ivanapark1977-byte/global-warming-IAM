1. Purpose and Design Philosophy

The dashboard is a first-order Integrated Assessment Model (IAM) — a single-region, annual-time-step simulation spanning 2024–2100. It couples four energy sectors, a GDP-damage economy, a simple climate physics block, endogenous carbon dioxide removal, and supply curve feedbacks for oil and LNG.

The design makes three deliberate trade-offs in favour of transparency:

  • Simplicity over completeness. Every equation is visible in the dashboard source code. No proprietary solver, no opaque model structure. A reader who understands basic algebra can trace any output back to its inputs.
  • Speed over fidelity. The model runs in milliseconds in a browser. This makes real-time slider interaction possible but rules out the iterative optimisation, vintage capital tracking, and spatial resolution of GCAM, MESSAGE-GLOBIOM, or REMIND.
  • Insight over precision. The primary output is a sense of which levers matter most and by how much, not a precise forecast. Outputs should be read as order-of-magnitude estimates with the uncertainty ranges in §10 of the technical documentation taken seriously.

The model is calibrated to reproduce IEA WEO 2025 and IPCC AR6 anchor points at 2024. Projected trajectories are deliberately cross-checked against IEA scenario families — but significant structural differences mean the model diverges from IEA results in predictable ways, discussed in detail in §11.

2. Interface Overview

The dashboard has three functional zones:

Every slider triggers an immediate full model re-run (2024–2100) on oninput. There is no "Run" button. Scenario preset buttons snap all sliders to a predefined configuration and re-run.

The summary bar at the bottom of the sidebar shows key 2100 outcomes for the current parameter set: temperature, CO₂ concentration equivalent, GDP index, sea level rise, and fleet electrification.

See §11 for a full comparison.

Interaction effects: The subsidy and deployment ceiling sliders interact. A high subsidy that makes renewables very cheap will not accelerate the transition beyond the deployment ceiling — grid infrastructure and permitting remain binding. Conversely, raising the ceiling without a subsidy only matters once renewables are already cheaper than fossil alternatives on a market basis.

5. Uncertainty Sliders

The four sidebar sliders represent forecast uncertainties — parameters whose true values are contested in the scientific or economic literature. Unlike policy sliders, they do not represent choices; they represent genuine unknowns. Each has a P10–central–P90 range derived from empirical evidence or IPCC assessments.

5.1 TCRE — Transient Climate Response to Cumulative Emissions

AttributeValue
Unit°C per 1,000 GtCO₂ (°C/TtCO₂)
Slider range0.26 to 0.78
Central value0.45
SourceIPCC AR6 WG1 §5.5 likely range; P10 = 0.26, P90 = 0.63 (extended to 0.78 in this model to cover the AR6 very likely range)

TCRE is the single most important uncertainty in the model, accounting for roughly 60% of 2100 temperature variance in No Policy runs. It encapsulates the cumulative carbon budget concept: total warming is approximately proportional to total cumulative CO₂ emissions, regardless of the emission rate. At P10 (0.26), the same No Policy cumulative emissions produce about 1.2°C less warming by 2100 than at P90 (0.78).

In revision 9, the model applies a non-linearity correction to TCRE above 2,000 GtCO₂ cumulative:

tcreCorrFactor = 1 + max(0, (netCumCO₂ − 2,000) / 15,000)

This correction acknowledges that linear TCRE underestimates warming at high cumulative emissions because Earth System feedbacks (permafrost thaw, reduced ocean uptake) amplify the temperature response. The correction adds up to +20% at 5,000 GtCO₂ — roughly +0.15 to 0.25°C in No Policy scenarios.

TCRE upper bound caveat: The IPCC AR6 very likely range for TCRE is 0.12–0.62 °C/TtCO₂, with the P95 at 0.63. The slider maximum of 0.78 was set at the prior release and extends slightly beyond the AR6 assessed range. Combined with the r9 non-linearity correction, the effective upper bound reaches ~0.87°C/TtCO₂ at high emission levels, exceeding IPCC's assessed 95th percentile. Users exploring tail risks should note that TCRE > 0.65 in this model represents a scenario outside the IPCC very likely range.

5.2 Transition Responsiveness

AttributeValue
UnitDimensionless multiplier on the switching rate equation
Slider range0.40 to 1.20
Central value0.70
SourceP10–P90 of technology adoption rate literature; reflects social, institutional, and infrastructure inertia

This parameter scales the speed at which each sector shifts fossil share in response to cost signals. It enters the switching equation as a direct multiplier: double the responsiveness, double the annual fossil share reduction for a given cost advantage. At P10 (0.40), economic cost advantages take twice as long to drive adoption as at the central value — consistent with historical lags in building retrofit or industrial process switching. At P90 (1.20), transitions are faster than the central assumption — consistent with a world where regulatory mandates, social norms, and infrastructure are already aligned with decarbonisation.

The effect of this slider is strongly amplified by carbon pricing: in No Policy, where cost advantages are small, varying responsiveness has a minor effect. In Strong Action, it is the second-largest driver of temperature uncertainty (22% of variance), because large carbon price signals are either quickly translated into switching (high responsiveness) or dissipated by institutional friction (low responsiveness).

This is the blended carbon intensity of fossil fuels across the Transport, Buildings, and Industry sectors. The range is relatively narrow (±8% of central), making this the smallest sensitivity in the OAT analysis. The distinction between the P10 and P90 values is meaningful:

  • P10 = 0.068: IEA standard baseline. Combustion-only accounting, no upstream methane leakage premium beyond the explicit LNG module.
  • P90 = 0.079: Includes higher upstream methane leakage (e.g. US shale gas or Permian flaring) at GWP-100. Values above 0.068 trigger an LNG methane leakage uplift in the CI calculation.

5.4 CDR Ceiling

AttributeValue
UnitGtCO₂/yr maximum removal rate at full scale
Slider range3.5 to 14
Central value7.0
SourceExpert elicitation on feasible CDR scale by 2100; IPCC AR6 WG3 range for CDR deployment scenarios

CDR (Carbon Dioxide Removal) only deploys when the carbon tax exceeds the CDR cost per tonne, which begins at $300/tCO₂ in 2050 and falls at 5%/yr thereafter. This means CDR is irrelevant in No Policy (carbon tax never exceeds the cost threshold) and only modestly relevant in Strong Action (it first crosses the cost threshold around 2053 and ramps over 30 years). The ceiling matters only once CDR is already deploying; in practice its contribution is 6% of temperature variance in Strong Action and effectively zero in No Policy.

The IEA NZE pathway assumes 7.6 GtCO₂/yr by 2050 — roughly three times what this model achieves by that date due to the 30-year ramp-up constraint. This is one of the primary reasons Strong Action gives ~2.1°C rather than the NZE's 1.5°C. See §11.

6. Damage Function Selector

Four options determine how temperature feeds back into GDP growth. All functions take ΔT = max(0, T − 1.29°C), i.e. warming above the 2024 observed level.

OptionFormula (damageFrac)GDP loss at 3°C total warmingFramework
IEA (none)0No damage feedbackIEA default — models economic pathways without explicit GDP-temperature feedback
Nordhaus0.01579·ΔT + 0.00362·ΔT²~−0.5%/yrDICE-2023 calibrated; the ×0.12 scalar is derived from this function
Medium0.02·ΔT + 0.015·ΔT²~−0.8%/yrBurke et al. (2015) central coefficients, DICE-style application
High-end†0.028·ΔT + 0.032·ΔT²~−1.2%/yrBurke et al. (2015) upper-bound coefficients, DICE-style application

The ×0.12 scalar that converts damageFrac to an annual GDP growth rate reduction is calibrated to the Nordhaus/DICE-2023 function. It is applied unchanged to Medium and High-end — an approximation, since those functions were not independently calibrated in this framework.

† High-end — important limitation: The High-end option uses Burke et al. (2015) coefficients but applies them as a DICE-style level effect on annual GDP growth. The original Burke empirical model estimates damages as a growth-rate effect, meaning losses compound into the capital stock year-on-year. This model's High-end therefore substantially understates the cumulative losses that a full Burke implementation would project — potentially by a factor of 3–5× over a 76-year horizon at high warming levels. Use High-end as an upper-bound sensitivity within the DICE paradigm, not as a replication of the Burke framework.

The "IEA (none)" option reproduces IEA's own practice: IEA WEO scenarios report economic pathways but exclude GDP-temperature damage feedbacks. Selecting this option makes the model's GDP trajectory directly comparable to IEA projections, but produces an optimistic picture of the economic cost of climate change.

9. Module Methodology

9.1 Energy Transition

Each sector's fossil share evolves annually according to a switching equation:

switch_s(t) = clamp( costAdv_s × COST_RESPONSE × RESP_s × frict_s , 0, maxDeploy_s )
fos_s(t+1) = max( floor_s, fos_s(t) − switch_s(t) )

The cost advantage signal is:

costAdv_s = max(0, (lcoe_fossil_s − lcoe_clean_s) / lcoe_fossil_s)

The friction term prevents switching from accelerating as the fossil share approaches its floor:

frict_s = min(1, (fos_s − floor_s) / range_s)

Clean technology costs follow Wright's Law (learning by doing):

lcoe_ren(t) = max(14, LCOE_REN₀ × (1 − learnRate)^doublings − renewSubsidy)

Doublings are counted from cumulative capacity deployed (endogenous). The learning rate slider (expressed in %/doubling) enters as (1 − learnRate/100)^doublings — it is a per-doubling rate following Wright's Law, not an annual rate. The model label shows %/doubling, but earlier documentation versions incorrectly labelled this as %/yr. This was corrected in r9.

At high renewable penetration, firming costs (storage, grid balancing) add a penetration-dependent premium:

lcoe_firmed(t) = lcoe_ren(t) + STORAGE_BASE × (1 − 0.18)^doublings × max(0, (renew − 0.15) / 0.35)^1.8

The industry sector uses a green hydrogen pathway with its own learning curve:

lcoe_H2(t) = H2_capex × (1 − 0.15)^doublings + lcoe_ren × 1.65 − h2Subsidy

9.2 Emissions

em_total = em_coal_pow + em_gas_pow + em_TBI − cdrRate
em_coal_pow = E_pow × coal_pow × 0.128 tCO₂/GJ
em_gas_pow = E_pow × gas_pow × 0.074 tCO₂/GJ
em_TBI = (E_trans × fos_trans + E_build × fos_build + E_ind × fos_ind) × CI_TBI_eff
CI_TBI_eff = CI_FOSSIL + lngShare × CI_LNG_PREMIUM × 0.3

The 0.3 factor accounts for the fact that only ~30% of T+B+I fossil energy is gas; the remainder is oil (transport) and coal (industry). Total energy in each sector is EI × GDP_abs × sector_weight.

9.3 Climate

Temperature tracks toward an equilibrium set by cumulative CO₂ and non-CO₂ forcing, with a 15-year ocean thermal lag:

T_eq(t) = TEMP₀ + nonCO₂_increment(t) + TCRE × netCumCO₂(t) × tcreCorrFactor(t)
T(t) = T(t−1) + (T_eq(t) − T(t−1)) / 15

Non-CO₂ forcing scales continuously with policy depth:

abateFrac = min(1, ctax_avg / 100)    [0 at no policy → 1.0 at ≥$100/tCO₂ average]
nonCO₂_increment = [nonCO₂_raw × abateFrac + max(0, nonCO₂_raw) × (1 − abateFrac)] + [min(0.30, t × 0.0039) × (1 − abateFrac)]

The first bracket is the abatement benefit (cooling as fossil share falls, scaled by policy depth); the second is the warming trend from fugitive emissions (rising with fossil output, suppressed by policy).

9.4 Economy and Damages

GDP(t+1) = GDP(t) × (1 + clamp(baseGDP − damageFrac × 0.12 − transDrag − cdrDrag, −5%, 9%))

Transition drag represents stranded asset write-downs and capital reallocation costs, declining over time as the system matures:

transDrag = transCost × switchRate / 0.01 × max(0, 1 − t/40)

CDR drag represents the resource cost of scaling up carbon removal technology at pace:

cdrDrag = cdrRate × CDR_cost(t) / (GDP_abs × 1e12)

9.5 Carbon Dioxide Removal

CDR deploys endogenously once the carbon tax exceeds its levelised cost:

CDR_cost(t) = 300 × (1 − 0.05)^(t−2050)    [active from 2050 only]
cdrRate(t) = min(1, (ctax − CDR_cost) / CDR_cost) × CDR_MAX × min(1, yearsDeployed / 30)

The 30-year ramp reflects supply chain and permitting constraints. CDR cannot exceed CDR_MAX (the ceiling slider). The cost-trigger mechanism means CDR is inactive below the threshold price, which in Strong Action is first crossed around 2053.

9.6 Supply Curve Feedbacks

The model's own energy demand drives oil and LNG prices through supply cost curve interpolation — prices are not exogenous scenario assumptions but emergent from the modelled demand trajectory. Oil demand is converted from the model's transport energy:

demand_mbpd = E_total × 0.22 × fos_trans × 0.4477 mb/d per EJ/yr

LNG prices derive from model LNG demand mapped onto a supply waterfall of 300+ individual project cost bars (LCOE by source region and technology). Clearing price is the cost of the marginal project needed to meet demand. Spot price benchmarks (JKM, TTF, HH) are derived from the JKM clearing price using fixed regional spreads.

9.7 Physical Impacts

Sea level rise follows the Rahmstorf semi-empirical rate model, calibrated to IPCC AR6 medians:

SLR(t) = 0.80 × Σ max(0, T(yr) − 1.29)    for yr = 2025..t    [cm above 2024]

The car fleet grows logistically toward a 2.20 billion vehicle saturation, with fossil and electric vehicles split by the transport fossil share.

SLR omits potential large-scale ice sheet instabilities (marine ice cliff instability, WAIS destabilisation), which could add 0.5–2 m under scenarios above 3°C. Our SLR projections should be considered conservative in high-warming cases.

10. Shortcomings and Simplifications

The following known limitations should inform how results are used and communicated. They are grouped by severity.

Critical structural simplifications

Single-region world. The model treats the planet as a single representative agent with uniform energy mix, carbon price, and technology access. In reality, OECD countries, China, and the Global South are on very different trajectories — China's coal trajectory alone differs sharply from the global average, and developing economy energy access growth is a distinct dynamic not captured here. Single-region aggregation can mask the distributional dimension of climate change (who causes it vs who suffers it) and overstates the coherence of a "world carbon price."

Damage function framework mismatch. All three damage function options apply a DICE-style level effect: damages reduce annual GDP growth but do not compound into the capital stock. The High-end coefficients are drawn from Burke et al. (2015), which is a growth-rate model where losses compound permanently. Using Burke coefficients in a DICE structure understates cumulative damages by a factor that grows with the time horizon — potentially 3–5× over 76 years at high warming. The model cannot faithfully implement Burke-style compounding damages without a more complex capital stock module.

Damages exclude the 2024 baseline. All damage functions condition on ΔT above 1.29°C — damages only accrue once warming exceeds today's already-elevated level. This omits the economic losses already occurring from observed warming (flooding, heat stress, agricultural disruption at 1.29°C). This is consistent with DICE methodology but understates total cumulative climate damages over 2024–2100 by roughly 0.1–0.3% of cumulative GDP.

Significant approximations

Linear TCRE. The Transient Climate Response to Cumulative Emissions is treated as approximately linear, which is a good approximation to ~2,000 GtCO₂ but underestimates warming at higher cumulative emissions due to non-linear Earth System feedbacks. The r9 correction factor partially addresses this but is itself a simplified parameterisation, not a full Earth System Model treatment.

No carbon cycle feedbacks. Permafrost thaw (estimated 0.03–0.04°C/°C of additional warming), reduced ocean CO₂ uptake, and tropical forest dieback are not explicitly modelled. At No Policy levels (>3°C), these feedbacks could add 0.2–0.5°C by 2100 beyond our projections.

No agricultural methane or land-use emissions. The non-CO₂ term is a simplified aggregate capturing the correlation between fossil fuel use and methane/N₂O emissions. Agricultural methane (livestock, rice) and land-use CO₂ (deforestation) are not separately modelled. In scenarios with rapid economic growth and dietary shifts, these emissions could diverge significantly from the model's assumption.

Static supply curves. The oil and LNG supply cost waterfalls do not evolve with resource depletion, technology change (e.g., methane pyrolysis, blue hydrogen), or geopolitical restructuring (except the War Mode shock). Long-run supply curve evolution is likely to affect gas competitiveness against hydrogen.

No tipping elements or catastrophic risks. Threshold responses (West Antarctic Ice Sheet collapse, Amazon dieback, permafrost abrupt thaw) that could trigger step-changes in the climate-economy system are not modelled. This means the model almost certainly understates tail risk — the probability of outcomes above 4°C is higher than shown.

Minor approximations

No behavioural demand reduction. The model does not capture modal shift (cars to transit), dietary change (less meat), or sufficiency behaviour. IEA NZE projects significant demand reduction from behaviour in transport and buildings; this model's demand is purely EI × GDP driven.

No critical mineral constraints. Battery supply chains, copper for electrification, rare earth elements for wind turbines — these physical supply constraints on the energy transition are not modelled. They could slow deployment beyond what the ceiling slider implies.

No trade or capital flows. Energy price signals do not trigger capital reallocation across regions, substitution of coal-heavy grid electricity for cleaner alternatives via interconnection, or technology transfer mechanisms.

Uniform carbon price within scenarios. Strong Action applies the same carbon price to all four sectors simultaneously, which is politically unrealistic. Sector-specific constraints (free allocation in industry, political resistance in transport) would reduce the real-world effectiveness of any uniform carbon price.

11. Divergence from IEA Scenarios

The IEA World Energy Outlook defines four primary scenarios that this model's presets are designed to approximate. Understanding where and why the model diverges from IEA is essential for using it correctly alongside WEO results.

11.2 Why Strong Action Does Not Reach 1.5°C

The IEA NZE pathway is constructed specifically to limit warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot. Our Strong Action scenario reaches approximately 2.1°C. The 0.6°C gap stems from five distinct structural differences:

(a) CDR scale and timing. This is the largest single driver of the gap. IEA NZE assumes 7.6 GtCO₂/yr of CDR by 2050, rising to ~9–10 GtCO₂/yr by 2080. In our model, CDR starts deploying only after the carbon tax exceeds $300/t (around 2053 in Strong Action), and then ramps over 30 years to a maximum of 7 GtCO₂/yr (central slider value). By 2100, our CDR has accumulated roughly 100–150 GtCO₂ of removal; IEA NZE accumulates over 500 GtCO₂. That 350+ GtCO₂ difference, multiplied by TCRE (0.45°C/TtCO₂), accounts for roughly 0.15–0.20°C of the gap. IEA's CDR assumptions depend heavily on BECCS (bioenergy with CCS) at land-use scales that most experts consider speculative and land-constrained.

(b) Carbon price trajectory and effective demand reduction. IEA NZE implies effective carbon prices of $130–250/t in advanced economies by 2030–2050, combined with regulatory mandates (EV bans, gas boiler bans, clean electricity standards) that constrain fossil use independent of price signals. Our Strong Action starts at $50/t and grows $7/yr, reaching $190/t by 2050 — broadly comparable in price level, but without any non-price mandates. IEA NZE's renewable deployment rate in power is close to 100% clean electricity in advanced economies by 2035, which our model cannot replicate because it relies on economic signals rather than regulatory mandates. This difference alone is responsible for perhaps 0.15°C.

(c) Effective TCRE. Our model uses the IPCC AR6 median TCRE of 0.45°C/TtCO₂. IEA's integrated modelling (using MAGICC or similar simple climate models coupled to IAMs) typically implies an effective TCRE of ~0.55°C/TtCO₂ because IEA's models include feedback amplifiers — permafrost carbon, aerosol adjustments, cloud feedbacks — that our first-order model omits. A higher effective TCRE means any given emission pathway requires more aggressive mitigation to hit the same temperature target. This asymmetry means that in a 1.5°C world, IEA achieves the target with a more aggressive mitigation pathway than ours, not because IEA is wrong but because IEA's climate model is more complete.

(d) Non-CO₂ gases. IEA NZE includes explicit modelling of methane from fossil fuel operations, agricultural methane, and industrial process gases (HFCs, SF₆, N₂O). These are targeted by specific regulatory measures in NZE — methane regulations for oil and gas, dietary shift, HFC phase-out. Our model captures only an aggregate non-CO₂ term that responds to average carbon price. It does not model the specific regulatory measures or the independent trajectory of agricultural methane.

(e) Demand-side behaviour and energy efficiency. IEA NZE projects significant demand reduction from modal shift (less private driving), dietary change (less meat, less food waste), and sufficiency behaviour (smaller homes, shared transport). These reduce total energy demand below what energy intensity improvement alone achieves. Our model's demand pathway is EI × GDP — energy intensity improves with carbon price and AEEI, but there is no explicit behavioural demand reduction separate from the EI term.

11.3 Why Current Policy Gives 2.9°C Rather Than IEA STEPS 2.7°C

The 0.2°C gap between our Current Policy and IEA STEPS has two causes:

Mandate-driven deployment. IEA STEPS incorporates EV purchase mandates, appliance efficiency standards, building codes, and grid connection requirements that directly constrain fossil demand beyond what carbon pricing alone achieves. For example, the EU and China have mandated effectively full EV sales shares by 2035, which in IEA's modelling drives rapid transport decarbonisation regardless of oil price. In our model, transport decarbonisation is driven entirely by the cost signal (oil price + carbon tax vs EV LCOE). This omission means our model underestimates the speed of technology adoption under stated policies.

Effective TCRE difference. As discussed above, IEA's climate model implies ~0.55°C/TtCO₂ while ours uses 0.45°C/TtCO₂. A higher TCRE means the same emissions path produces more warming, which means hitting the same 2.7°C in STEPS requires somewhat fewer cumulative emissions than our model implies — or conversely, that IEA's STEPS pathway produces more warming than a naive reading might suggest. The IEA STEPS figure of 2.7°C already accounts for the higher effective TCRE.

11.5 Appropriate Use Cases Given These Divergences

Given the structural differences above, the model is well-suited for:

  • Understanding which uncertain parameters drive the most temperature uncertainty (TCRE dominates)
  • Exploring how policy levers interact — e.g. whether subsidy or carbon price is more effective given a specific technology cost trajectory
  • Illustrating the feedback between oil demand, LNG prices, and the energy transition speed
  • Comparing the relative magnitude of policy vs uncertainty in determining 2100 outcomes
  • Teaching the structural mechanics of climate-economy modelling with full code transparency

It is not appropriate for:

  • Projecting country or regional outcomes — the single-region assumption is too strong
  • Claiming that Strong Action achieves 1.5°C — it does not, and the gap is structural, not a calibration error
  • Quantifying the economic benefits of climate action with the High-end damage function — the DICE-style implementation substantially understates Burke-framework damages
  • Policy analysis requiring technology vintage tracking, critical mineral constraints, or detailed financial flow modelling

Recommended practice: Use this model alongside — not instead of — IEA WEO results. It adds value by making the sensitivity to TCRE and technology learning rates explicit in real time, and by revealing the role of oil and LNG supply curve feedbacks that IEA's fixed-price scenarios obscure. Cross-check headline temperature numbers against IEA, and flag the structural gaps noted in §11.2–11.3 when communicating results.
